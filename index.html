Revisiting the Relationship between Continuous Integration and Software Quality: An Empirical Study


PAPER INFORMATIONS

In this work, we use data from 75 GitHub projects to analyze whetherprojects improve their quality inspection outcomes after CI adoption. Furthermore, we use data from other 184 GitHub projects to analyze whether the degree of the adoption of certain CI practices improves the Quality Inspection outocmes of the projects. Finally, we use a subset of 96 Java projects from the aforementioned 184 projects, to analyze whether the degree of adoption of proper CI practices shares a strong association with improved Object-Oriented Quality outcomes.

In particular, we address the following research questions:

RQ1: Does the adoption of CI share a relationship with improved quality inspection outcomes? Surprisingly, our statistical analyses reveal that adopting Travis CI has little to no association to improved Quality Inspection outcomes.

See the Violin plots for the comparisons performed in RQ1



RQ2: Does the degree of CI adoption share a relationship with improved quality inspection outcomes? By analyzing four CI practices---avoiding long-running builds, fixing broken builds as soon as possible; controlling for acceptable test coverage, and integrating at least daily---our results reveal that only controlling for acceptable test coverage and avoiding long-running builds have a strong association with improved quality inspection outcomes.

See the Violin plots for the comparisons performed in RQ2



RQ3: Does the degree of CI adoption share a relationship with improved OO quality outcomes? Our results reveal that the practice of integrating at least daily has little to no association with improved OO quality outcomes. Surprisingly, we observe that better OO quality outcomes are strongly associated with longer build durations and lower test coverage. However, better OO quality outcomes are also strongly associated with quicker build fixes.

See the Violin plots for the comparisons performed in RQ3
